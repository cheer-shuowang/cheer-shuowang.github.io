About

Algorithmic Selection and Value Alienation in the Esports Labor Market

Project  ·  In progress  ·  Participant, with Runyi Ma and Yining Fang

In esports, data is not a description of the player — it is the player's "digital body." Entry into the professional labor market runs not through a résumé but through high-frequency, real-time signals: ladder rank, win rate, and MMR/ELO. This project asks whether that algorithmic "digital meritocracy" delivers a fairer path into the profession, or builds a colder, more extractive cage around it.

Questions

We separate three often-conflated processes — entry (eligibility to turn professional), selection (which team, what contract), and promotion (from rookie to established veteran) — and trace how a player's "value" migrates from pure competitive productivity into commercial worth: transfers, endorsements, streaming. We then ask whether the system produces "digital alienation" — career burnout, and an "algorithmic lock-in" in which one major dip in the data forecloses future opportunities even when the player has not physically declined.

Approach

We build digital portraits of roughly 100 professional League of Legends players (newcomers vs. veterans) from multi-source public data — Leaguepedia, Liquipedia, OP.GG, Esports Earnings. Logistic regression tests the ladder-percentile threshold for entry; linear models link in-game KPIs to market value; and survival analysis (Kaplan–Meier) estimates career "half-life" and lock-in effects.

Output

An empirically grounded "digital-meritocracy" evaluation index, a working paper on algorithmic discipline and value alienation in the esports labor market, and policy proposals — such as a "human margin" and "dynamic fault-tolerance" — to keep automated selection from hardening into a data-only regime.

Playing Heaven: Remapping Early Modern Neo-Confucian Worlds with AI

Project  ·  Part of the Schmidt Sciences Humanities & AI Virtual Institute (HAVI), 2025
Advised by Prof. Yumeng Hou (NUS)  ·  PIs: Javier Cha (HKU), Michael Chung (HKUST), Yumeng Hou & Miguel Escobar Varela (NUS)

An inter-university Hong Kong–Singapore project building DeepPast, a transformer-based AI research assistant for low-resource early-modern East Asian texts, to remap the intellectual history of Neo-Confucianism. My contribution spans two workstreams.

Dataset Construction
A vision-language-model (Qwen3-VL) pipeline that extracts structured information from historical source texts — illustrations, postures, and transcriptions — across a metadata → page-indexing → extraction → human-verification workflow. Code ↗
Semantic Change
A computational semantic analysis of the "Neo-Confucianization" of Wang Yangming's military discourse. Using contextualized embeddings (SikuRoBERTa) with representational similarity analysis and baseline subtraction over an 18-source corpus (3rd century – Qing; five registers, from Sunzi commentaries to martial-arts treatises), we track eight core concepts (形, 勢, 心, 氣, 正, 奇, 虛, 實) along diachronic and synchronic axes. Findings: the Neo-Confucian uptake is selective — confined to moral-cosmological concepts and the literati register, absent from tactical vocabulary — and 氣/心 undergo a sharp "embodiment" turn at the Qing martial-arts stage. Working paper in preparation.

Institution, Context, and Action: Crisis Evolution in China's Major Disaster Accidents

Bachelor's Thesis  ·  Ocean University of China  ·  advised by Xue Wang  ·  2025

Existing disaster research tends to isolate single factors. This thesis instead builds a three-dimensional "institution–context–action" framework to explain how crises evolve, applying LDA topic modeling to 30 major-disaster investigation reports released by China's Ministry of Emergency Management (2010–2025), combined with case studies.

Three dimensions

Institutional factors form a "systemic risk chain" through pre-event monitoring gaps, mid-event coordination failures, and post-event accountability softening — exemplified by the 2015 Tianjin Port explosion. Contextual factors amplify impact through natural vulnerability and social sensitivity, as in the 2021 Zhengzhou "7·20" rainstorm. Action factors — bureaucratic inertia, under-investment in safety, and weak public risk awareness — drove failures such as the 2019 Xiangshui chemical explosion.

Contribution

Integrating quantitative text mining with qualitative case analysis, the thesis argues that institutional defects define the baseline of prevention, contextual vulnerabilities shape the paths of risk transmission, and action biases move the threshold of loss-of-control — and derives full-cycle resilience-governance implications across all three levels.
